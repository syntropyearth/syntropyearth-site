THE OBLIGATION

Why this is on your desk right now depends on where you sit. That’s the point.

No domestic law in your operating market may require this spend. The obligation comes down from a parent headquarters or corporate group instead -- a global access commitment, a sustainability pledge, a target with a reporting date already attached to it.

The local or regional access and sustainability function’s job is to turn that global promise into a measured outcome headquarters can stand behind, in markets whose specific climate exposure and access barriers they were never built to track.

You are already spending, and already reporting. The pressure now is whether what you report survives assurance -- BRSR Core assurance extending across India’s largest listed companies, and equivalent scrutiny wherever your group discloses.

Most access programmes were built to show reach, not to survive an assurer asking how that reach was measured during a disrupted season. Retrofitting defensible measurement after the fact costs far more than designing it in from the start.

This obligation runs through your own field force and delivery workforce, not a community programme. Medical representatives, community health workers, distribution and cold-chain staff working through heat, flood and storm are becoming a named line of employer responsibility.

The buyer here is whoever answers for that workforce when a season goes badly -- and needs the answer to be designed in advance, not improvised after the fact.

Whichever door you came through, the underlying question is the same: does your access programme still work when the climate does not cooperate?
THE GAP

Access reporting shows an annual average. Climate stress does not arrive as an average.

A programme that reaches eighty percent of its target population across the year can still fail sixty percent of them in the exact month the monsoon peaks. The annual figure is technically true and practically misleading -- it hides the population your programme is actually failing, and hides it in the months that matter most.

This is not a hypothetical gap. It is a measurement design choice: track reach by season and by geography, or report a number that flatters the programme while the people most exposed to climate stress quietly fall out of it.

THE METHOD · ACCESS BARRIERS, RECONSIDERED

Every access programme is designed against four barriers. Climate adds a fifth.

Availability, affordability, awareness, accessibility -- the four barriers most access programmes already track. Climate change is creating a fifth that almost none of them account for: whether the physical route between the patient and the product survives the season.

01

Availability

Is the product stocked at the right facility, at the right time.

02

Affordability

Can the patient pay the cost of treatment.

03

Awareness

Does the patient know about the condition and the treatment; does the provider know to prescribe.

04

Accessibility

Can the patient physically and administratively reach care.

05

Reachability -- the barrier climate adds

Flood, heat and storm disrupt the physical infrastructure between patient and care, independent of whether the first four barriers were solved. A programme can be affordable, available, well-known and formally accessible, and still fail the moment the road to the clinic floods.

Programmes that track the four classic barriers but not reachability are systematically underestimating their own access failure -- and reporting confidence they have not earned.
THE METHOD · WHERE THE FIFTH BARRIER SHOWS UP

Four places climate stress breaks delivery, mapped to how your programme actually runs.

01

Geographic access

Hub-and-spoke models expose their spokes first -- peripheral facilities and last-mile delivery points are the most climate-exposed part of any network.

02

Supply chain integrity

The CNF-to-stockist-to-retailer layer is where climate-driven stockouts originate, long before they surface as a reported access failure. Cold chain is the sharpest exposure of all.

03

Medical representatives, community health workers and pharmacists carry the direct physical burden -- they travel in the exact conditions that block patients.

04

Financial & demand continuity

Household income and out-of-pocket capacity fall with the same climate shocks that disrupt supply -- demand and delivery can break simultaneously, not separately.

HEALTH EQUITY, DESIGNED PROPERLY

Equity is not equal access. It is proportionally more support where the barriers are highest.

A programme that reaches the same number of patients in every geography is not equitable if some of those geographies carry a far higher disease burden and a far higher climate exposure. Design principles that make a health equity programme genuinely defensible:

Disaggregate by climate vulnerability

Track outcomes separately for flood zones, high-heat areas and areas with poor physical access. Averages mask exactly the gap that matters.

Weight allocation to highest burden

Geographic prioritisation should follow climate vulnerability mapping, not just where the existing infrastructure happens to sit.

Measure access loss during events

The reach figure during the worst month of the year is the equity measure that matters -- not the annual average that absorbs it.

Reach the workers who deliver it too

Community health workers and field staff need active protection built into the programme, not just passive facility availability for the patients they serve.

Where this decision sits varies by organisation -- inside a Health Equity function where one exists, or straddling CSR and market access where it does not. Either way, it is rarely the same desk that owns commercial detailing, and the programme is stronger when it is designed with that separation in mind.
THE SDG CONNECTION

Only 15% of SDG targets are on track. Health and climate are two of the furthest behind.

Most sustainability reports claim alignment with five to eight SDGs, usually as a logo table with no causal chain behind it. The connection that actually matters for this work is specific: climate change (SDG 13) is the single greatest systemic threat to health progress (SDG 3), and the burden of that threat falls hardest exactly where SDG 10 says it will.

3SDG 3

Good health and well-being

Climate threatens nearly every SDG 3 target -- raising infectious disease burden, NCD risk from heat and air quality, and straining the health-system capacity universal coverage depends on.

13SDG 13

Climate action

The most directly relevant goal, and among the most off-track globally. Programme-level resilience data is one of the few places a company can show real, measured contribution.

10SDG 10

Reduced inequalities

Climate change widens inequality -- the populations with the least capacity to adapt carry the heaviest health burden from it. This is the equity lens applied at goal level.

1SDG 1

No poverty

Poverty is the root determinant of both health vulnerability and climate exposure -- reduced capacity to adapt compounds every other barrier.

6SDG 6

Clean water and sanitation

Flooding and drought drive waterborne disease -- a direct SDG 3-SDG 6 intersection your access data can capture.

11SDG 11

Sustainable cities and communities

Urban heat islands and urban flooding sit here -- acute for last-mile delivery into dense informal settlements.

Better practice names the specific target, the programme activity addressing it, and the outcome metric that proves the contribution -- not just the goal-level logo. That specificity is what CAST is built to produce.
