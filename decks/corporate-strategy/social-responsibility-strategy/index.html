THE OBLIGATION

Why this is on your desk right now depends on where you sit. That's the point.

Section 135 of the Companies Act requires a fixed share of average net profit to move into CSR every financial year, once a company crosses set thresholds of turnover, net worth, or profit.

The spend is not optional. The only real choice left is what it buys: activity that photographs well, or a programme built to survive a board question, an auditor, and a bad monsoon.

No domestic law in your operating market may require this spend. The obligation instead comes down from a parent headquarters or corporate group -- a net-zero pledge, an ESG commitment, a target with a reporting date attached.

The local or regional team's job is to turn that global promise into a measured outcome headquarters can actually stand behind, in a market whose specific obligations they weren't built to track.

This obligation runs through your own workforce, not a community programme. Heat exposure, health access, and safety for a distributed team are becoming a named line of employer responsibility, not just an HR policy.

The buyer here is not the CSR head. It's whoever answers for the workforce when a season goes badly, and needs that answer to be designed, not improvised.

You're already spending. The pressure now is whether you can defend what you report -- BRSR assurance in India, GRI or an equivalent standard elsewhere, either way a disclosure someone external will eventually check.

Most programmes were never built with that scrutiny in mind. Retrofitting defensible measurement after the fact is far more expensive than designing one in from the start.

In plenty of organisations, nobody owns this yet. Social responsibility sits inside HR, or marketing, or wherever it landed first -- disconnected from strategy, and unmeasured.

That absence is the opening. Design it right from the start, and it never needs retrofitting once someone finally asks who owns it.

THE GAP

Most programmes are reported by activity. Almost none are reported by resilience.

What gets measured today

People reached. Sessions run. Funds disbursed. Numbers that describe effort, not durability.

A count of two thousand beneficiaries says nothing about whether the programme still runs if the delivery partner loses its funding next year.

What almost never gets measured

What happens to the programme when the thing it depends on breaks: a partner loses a grant, site access changes, a budget line gets cut mid-year.

None of that shows up in a report built to describe what happened, not what would happen next.

Inside health and life sciences, that gap has a sharper shape.

What gets measured today

Clinics visited. Camps run. Beneficiaries counted. Numbers that describe effort, not durability.

A count of forty camps run says nothing about whether even one could operate during last year's flood.

What almost never gets measured

What happens to patient access when a flood closes the road, a heatwave shuts the clinic for a week, or a cold-chain route drops out for a month.

None of that shows up in a report built to describe what happened, not what would happen next.

That gap stays invisible until the year it's tested. Then it's the line nobody can defend.

HOW IT RUNS

Four to eight weeks, depending on programme scope.

PHASE 1 · WEEKS 1–2

Diagnose

Stakeholder interviews across the teams that touch the programme today. A review of the current strategy, past reports, and existing partner agreements. The four-dimension scoring runs against what's actually there, not what the annual report says is there.

PHASE 2 · WEEKS 3–5

Design

The theory of change gets drafted and pressure-tested against your real constraints. Partner criteria and the measurement design get built alongside your team, not handed over cold -- working sessions catch what only your team would know.

PHASE 3 · WEEKS 6–8

Hand over

The board deck and three-year roadmap get finalised. A working session walks your team through delivery and answers the harder questions -- a partner underperforming, a budget shifting, a season turning -- before anyone signs off.

After the design closes

Ongoing strategic support is available on a monthly retainer -- watching the programme through the season, adjusting the plan as conditions change, and keeping the measurement current between formal reviews. Not delivery. Continued strategic oversight.

WHERE WE STOP

A design partner, not a delivery team.

We design the strategy, the theory of change, the partner criteria, and the measurement itself. We do not run the programme day to day, deliver it on the ground, or replace your implementing partners -- that work sits with them, and it should.
